    Hollowknight Silksong; The Crash

    The long-awaited release of Hollow Knight: Silksong on September 4, 2025, sparked an unprecedented surge of global demand that temporarily brought down multiple online gaming storefronts. Developed by the three-person studio Team Cherry, Silksong was initially conceived as downloadable content for 2017’s Hollow Knight before growing into a full sequel.

    “It soon became too large and too unique to stay a DLC, as Hornet’s adventure kept getting bigger and bigger,”
    Team Cherry explained when the project was first revealed. The original game, a hand-drawn action-platformer, sold over 15 million copies worldwide, earning critical acclaim for its tight gameplay, atmospheric world, and intricate level design. Its combination of challenging combat, exploration-driven progression, and rich lore captivated players across the globe, creating one of the most dedicated indie fan bases in recent years. Fans organized online communities, fan art, speedrunning competitions, and lore discussions, all of which helped sustain excitement for a sequel over nearly a decade. The game’s enduring popularity and influence on indie game design set a high bar that Silksong was tasked with meeting, heightening anticipation for the release.

    Digital storefronts buckle under demand

    When Silksong went live across Steam, Nintendo eShop, PlayStation Store, and Xbox Store, players rushed to download the game. Unlike many modern releases, Silksong did not allow preorders or preloads, meaning millions attempted to purchase and install it at the same time. The result was widespread disruption. Steam’s store page repeatedly failed to load, checkout systems crashed, and error messages spread quickly across social media. Nintendo’s eShop, Sony’s PlayStation Store, and Microsoft’s Xbox Store also reported outages and long delays.

    Record-breaking player counts

    Despite the chaos, Silksong set records almost immediately. Within just 30 minutes of release, the game registered over 110,000 concurrent players on Steam. Within hours, the number climbed to around 454,000 concurrent users, securing a spot among the platform’s most played games. The overwhelming demand even led some independent studios to delay their own releases, acknowledging they couldn’t compete for visibility during the frenzy.

    A new chapter for indie success

    The launch of Silksong highlights the extraordinary reach of independent games. A project crafted by a small Australian team briefly destabilized the world’s largest gaming platforms — a milestone usually associated with blockbuster releases from billion-dollar publishers. The success of Silksong signals more than just strong sales; it marks a turning point for indie gaming in the digital age. Even with technical hiccups, the game’s global popularity underscores the power of community-driven anticipation and hand-crafted design. For Team Cherry, the launch confirms that small studios can not only compete but dominate in a marketplace often thought to be reserved for industry giants.

    Key Takeaways

    1. Infrastructure gaps: Major storefronts still struggle to handle high-demand launches, even when the game comes from a small studio.
    2. Preorder trade-offs: Skipping preorders and preloads intensified launch stress, creating both anticipation and technical headaches.
    3. Indie dominance: Silksong demonstrates that independent studios can command global attention on par with AAA giants.
    4. Ripple effects: Competing developers altered their release schedules, showing how one game can reshape the wider gaming calendar.

    Glossary of Terms

    DLC (Downloadable Content)
    Additional content for an existing game that can be downloaded separately, often adding new storylines, characters, or features. Silksong was originally intended as DLC before becoming a full sequel.
    Concurrent Players
    The number of players online and actively playing a game at the same time. Silksong reached approximately 454,000 concurrent users on Steam at peak.
    AAA Game
    A high-budget, high-profile game produced by a large studio, often contrasted with indie titles in terms of scale and marketing.
